World Cultural Diversity Day · 21 May 2026

Would you recognise it?

Five real situations from the store floor. For each one, choose your answer — then find out what's really happening.

Your answers are not saved or tracked. This is a self-discovery exercise — just for you.

01 of 05

The customer who "speaks so well"

A customer walks into the store. You help them as usual. As they leave, your colleague says: "Wow, your Spanish is so good!"

What's happening here?

A A genuine compliment
B An assumption about their origin based on how they look
C It depends on the context
B — and also C

The comment assumes that person shouldn't speak Spanish well, based on how they look. Even if the intention is kind, the underlying message is: "I didn't expect this from someone like you."

Ammar

"They look at you and they're like: 'Why are you speaking English in my country?'" — the same logic, in reverse.

In the store: With customers, let them decide how they want to communicate. With colleagues, avoid comments that treat someone's language or accent as surprising. Speaking a language well is not an exception — it's simply who that person is.

02 of 05

The new colleague

A new colleague joins the team. She is from Indonesia, like someone else on the team you have known for years. In her first week, you say: "I'm sure you and your colleague will have loads in common."

Is this a problem?

A No — it's a way of helping her settle in
B Yes — you're assuming they share the same personality because they share the same origin
C Only if she takes it the wrong way
B

Sharing a nationality does not mean sharing an identity, a working style or a way of relating to others. And Natasha recognises it in herself — no one is immune.

Natasha

"I expected her to work the same way. We have the same thoughts. And then I'm like: 'This is different.' I am also a little bit biased."

In the store: When someone new joins, start from zero. Don't assign them a starting point based on their origin — even a positive one. Let them be the one to tell you who they are.

03 of 05

The list of countries

A customer who appears to be Asian walks into the store. Your colleague, with good intentions, wants to connect and asks: "Are you from Japan? Or China? Korea maybe?"

What would you do?

A The same — it's a natural way to connect
B Wait and let the customer share if they want to
C Ask more openly: "Where are you from?"
B

Curiosity is valid. The way it's expressed is what makes the difference.

Natasha

"They give me a whole list of countries. Chinese, Korean, Japan? I haven't even had time to answer."

Vaia

"There was a girl that she looked exactly like you." — Assuming two people look alike because of shared physical features is the same mechanism.

In the store: If the conversation reaches that point naturally, an open question is always better than a list. Most importantly — don't assume you can guess someone's origin before they share it. Let them decide what they want to tell you.

04 of 05

The gesture that gets called out

During the morning meeting, a colleague from India nods as he listens. Someone on the team jokes: "Always nodding! Do you just agree with everything?"

Is it just a joke?

A Yes — there's no bad intention behind it
B No — it singles out a cultural gesture as if it were unusual
C It depends on how well the team knows each other
B

A completely natural gesture in one culture becomes, without anyone meaning it, something that needs to be explained or justified. The gestural norm of the majority becomes the norm for everyone.

Ammar

"Why are you nodding your head so much? It might come across as very not-so-nice when you say it."

In the store: If a colleague's communication style feels unfamiliar, don't call it out in front of the team. If it genuinely affects the work, address it privately and with curiosity — not correction. There are many valid ways to listen, respond and participate.

05 of 05

"But where are you really from?"

A colleague tells you she has lived in Spain for 11 years. You ask: "Yes, but where are you originally from?"

What are you unknowingly telling her?

A That you have genuine curiosity about her story
B That you don't see her as someone who belongs here
C Both things at the same time
C — and that is exactly the complexity

The curiosity is real — and it's valid. But the question tells that person their answer wasn't enough. That there is something about them that doesn't fit with who they say they are.

Brenda

"Having been on the other side, I know what it feels like. When I meet anyone, I don't judge. I ask."

Brenda

"I go back to my country and it's like: 'You're not really from here anymore. We don't feel you're one of us.'"

In the store: When someone tells you where they are from, that is their answer. If they want to share more of their story over time, they will. It's not your question that opens that door — it's the trust you build together every day.

What we take away

Awareness is the first step.

None of these situations require bad intention to cause harm. That is precisely what makes them hard to spot — and what makes them worth talking about today.

Brenda

"Having been on the other side, I know what it feels like. When I meet anyone, I don't judge. I ask."

Natasha

"We have to be very careful not to be biased to people — to take away the stereotype of people."

It's asking before assuming. It's letting the person in front of you tell you who they are, instead of deciding before they speak. It's recognising that our teams and our customers deserve that space — every day, not just today.
